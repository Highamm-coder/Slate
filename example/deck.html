The fuller picture Section 02

What we're actually
solving for.

The surface problem

On paper, the narrative is already there. The product is real, the positioning is articulate, and the team can describe what they do to anyone who asks in the hallway. But the story doesn't travel. It depends on the person telling it — their room-reading, their shorthand, their ability to adapt in real time.

The artefacts haven't kept up. Decks get rebuilt from scratch every quarter. Sales, marketing, and product each stage slightly different versions. The audience hears a confident answer in one meeting and a cautious one in the next, and slowly starts to weight both lower.

The real problem underneath

A story only compounds when it's portable. It has to travel without the person who wrote it in the room, into decks and docs and one-pagers that everyone trusts enough to reuse. That's the shift this work is after — from a story the founders tell to a story the company carries.

The bar isn't cleverness or novelty. It's clarity that holds under pressure: a partner reading the deck cold on a Sunday night, a new hire opening it in week two, a reporter scanning it before a call. Each one has to arrive at the same idea, in roughly the same words, without anyone coaching them through it.

How we work Section 03

Five stages, built around decisions.

01 · Listen

Interview the system, not just the leadership

We talk to founders, operators, frontline sellers, and two or three customers who know you well. The goal isn't a transcript — it's a pattern. Where does the story already work without coaching? Where does it quietly fall apart? We come back with the signal, not the noise.

02 · Frame

Write the argument before we design the page

Before a single slide gets styled, we put the core argument on a page of prose. One premise, three claims, and the evidence that backs each one. If the prose holds up, the deck will. If it doesn't, no amount of layout will hide it — so we fix it here, in the cheapest possible medium.

03 · Shape

Design the spine, then the surface

We build a skeleton deck — structure, hierarchy, pacing — and review it with you before any visual polish lands. You see how the story moves from slide to slide, where the audience pauses, where they accelerate. Feedback at this stage costs a conversation, not a rebuild.

04 · Finish

Resolve every detail to presentation grade

Typography, spacing, data visualisation, and motion all get tuned at this stage. We proof it on the screens and rooms you'll actually present in — boardrooms, Zoom, investor docs exported as PDF — so nothing surprises you when it matters.

05 · Hand-off

Leave the system in a state your team can run

The final artefact is a deck that works, plus the components, tokens, and written guidance your team needs to extend it without us. We'd rather you never need a retainer than one that quietly grows every quarter.

Selected work Section 04

How this played out
for a recent partner.

Series B fintech, rebuilding the investor narrative

The company had closed a strong Series A off the back of product momentum, but the pitch that worked at seven million in ARR was beginning to creak at twenty. The team was improvising in every meeting — answering the same three objections from scratch, re-drawing the same category diagram on whiteboards, losing the thread on what they actually wanted the round to fund.

We spent two weeks inside the business — operators, customers, the last six lost deals — then wrote a single-page argument that held the whole pitch together. From there, the deck, the data room one-pager, and the memo template all fell out of the same spine.

The round closed in under nine weeks from first partner meeting to term sheet, over-subscribed. More importantly, the team reports that the story has held through the first two board cycles since — the artefact is still doing the work without being rebuilt.
